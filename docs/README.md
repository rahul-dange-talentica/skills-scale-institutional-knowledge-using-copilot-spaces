# OctoAcme Project Management Docs

Welcome to OctoAcme Project Management Docs! This README provides an overview of our project management framework and a convenient index of every process document in this folder. Whether you are a new team member onboarding or a stakeholder looking for a specific process, this is your starting point for navigating our documentation.

## Project Management Processes Overview

OctoAcme follows a structured, iterative project lifecycle built on core principles of customer-first delivery, clear ownership, and psychological safety. Every project moves through well-defined phases — initiation, planning, execution, release, and retrospective — each with its own artifacts, checkpoints, and responsibilities. The lifecycle begins with a Project Charter that captures the problem statement, stakeholders, and high-level timeline, and concludes with retrospective notes and action items that feed continuous improvement into the next cycle.

Roles and personas are clearly defined across all projects. The Project Manager (PM) coordinates delivery, schedules, risks, and communications; the Product Manager (PdM) owns the product vision, backlog prioritization, and success metrics; Developers design, build, and test software components; and QA validates quality and acceptance criteria. Stakeholders provide inputs and approvals throughout the lifecycle. Detailed responsibilities, goals, and typical communication patterns for each role are captured in the Roles & Personas document.

Communication follows a consistent cadence: weekly syncs between the PM and PdM, twice-weekly standups for the delivery team, monthly stakeholder updates, and ad-hoc escalations as needed. Risk management is continuous — risks are identified during planning and execution, assessed by impact and likelihood, tracked in a Risk Register, and reviewed at weekly syncs. Stakeholder communication uses a single source of truth for status, with defined templates for weekly updates and incident communications.

Quality assurance and release practices are standardized to reduce risk and improve observability. Before any release, all acceptance criteria must be met, CI and security scans must pass, release notes must be drafted, and rollback plans must be documented. Releases are categorized as patch, minor, or major, and deployments follow a structured checklist that includes staging smoke tests, production deployment via an automated pipeline, post-deploy verification, and stakeholder announcements. After each release or project phase, the team conducts a blameless retrospective to capture learnings and drive continuous improvement.
